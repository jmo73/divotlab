Start here New to this category? Begin with Feet Together Driver to calibrate tempo, then move to the Corridor Drill once your balance is consistent. High handicaps should spend 80% of driving practice on these two before adding others.

Setup

  • Ball position off lead heel, driver teed so equator sits at crown
  • Feet shoulder-width or slightly wider, trail foot flared 15 degrees
  • Spine tilted 5 degrees away from target, weight 55% on trail side
  • Grip pressure 4/10 — firm enough to control, light enough to release

Feels

  • Wide takeaway — feel the left shoulder stretching under the chin
  • Pressure shift to trail heel on backswing, then drive into lead heel on downswing
  • Shallow the club in the slot — feel it dropping behind you, not over the top
  • Explode through impact, chest rotating fast to face the target at follow-through

Avoid

  • Early extension — standing up out of posture through impact kills power
  • Over-swinging — speed comes from sequence, not effort
  • Casting from the top — letting the wrists unhinge before the body leads
  • Aiming body at the target instead of parallel left of the target line

Drills

Driving
Balls20
Time20 min
LocationRange

Find two visual targets 20 yards apart at 200 yards, forming a corridor. Every shot must land inside. Trains a repeatable, controlled start line and removes the urge to go max effort at the expense of direction.

Steps

  1. Set two alignment rods 20 yards apart at your comfortable carry distance
  2. Hit 5 shots focusing only on hitting the corridor, not distance
  3. Track how many of 20 balls land between the rods
  4. Narrow the corridor by 5 yards once you hit 16/20
Benchmark16 of 20 balls in a 20-yard corridor before narrowing. Scratch: 18/20 in a 12-yard corridor.
Balls15
Time15 min
LocationRange

Pick three distinct targets — left edge, center, right edge. Hit 5 balls at each in sequence. Forces intentional alignment changes and proves you can shape or redirect the start line on demand.

Steps

  1. Identify three targets at least 20 yards apart
  2. Go through your full pre-shot routine before each ball
  3. Hit 5 to the left target, 5 to center, 5 to the right
  4. Grade yourself: how many of 15 ended up on the correct side?
Benchmark12 of 15 on the correct side. High HC: 9/15 acceptable to start.
Balls20
Time20 min
LocationRange

Hit every driver at 80% perceived effort. No tempo rush, no killing it. For high and mid handicaps, the biggest distance gains come from better contact — not more clubhead speed. This drill trains that habit.

Steps

  1. Before each swing, say "80%" aloud or to yourself
  2. Focus on a smooth transition — no rushing from the top
  3. Track carry distance vs. your max-effort average
  4. Most players find 80% effort produces 95% of distance with better dispersion
BenchmarkConsistent contact on 16/20 balls. Less than 10% distance drop vs. max swing.
Balls15
Time12 min
LocationRange

Stand with feet together and hit driver at 70% effort. Eliminates the ability to overuse the lower body, forcing sequencing from the core and arms. Any balance loss means you rushed the transition.

Steps

  1. Place feet together, touching at the heels
  2. Make a controlled backswing — balance is the priority
  3. If you fall off balance, slow down more
  4. Alternate: 2 feet-together, then 1 normal swing to transfer the feel
BenchmarkFinish in balance on 13/15 swings. If falling off, reduce swing length before adding effort.
Balls18
Time20 min
LocationRange

Hit 6 shots at three tee heights: low (just above turf), normal, and high. Trains attack angle awareness. A low tee demands a shallower path; a high tee rewards an ascending strike. Reveals what your natural swing does.

Steps

  1. Tee 6 balls low (1/4 inch), 6 at normal height, 6 at high (1.5 inch)
  2. Note where contact lands on the face for each group
  3. Low tee: aim for solid strike, not height. High tee: launch it
  4. Use launch monitor if available to track attack angle changes
BenchmarkSolid contact on 5/6 at each height. Attack angle should shift +2 to +4 degrees from low to high tee.

LocationRange

Hit 3 balls in a row to the same target. Then 2 in a row. Then 1 that counts. The single ball carries the weight of the whole set — if you miss, start over from 3. Simulates tee shot pressure in a competition round.

Steps

  1. Pick one specific target, not a zone
  2. Hit 3 balls with the full routine. All 3 must land on target side
  3. Then 2 balls — both must land on target side
  4. Then 1 ball. Miss it, restart the whole sequence

Hit 20 balls with a launch monitor tracking carry distance. Calculate your average, remove the 2 outliers on each end, and find your reliable carry. This becomes your course management number — the distance you can count on 80% of the time.

Steps

  2. Remove the 2 longest and 2 shortest

Balls15
Time20 min
LocationRange

Alternate between maximum effort swings and 80% swings in sets of 3. The contrast trains the nervous system to access higher speed while maintaining mechanics. Used by tour players to build clubhead speed without breaking down the pattern.

Steps

  1. Hit 3 balls at absolute maximum effort (ignore direction)
  2. Immediately hit 3 balls at 80% — feel how much easier it is
  3. Repeat 3 cycles, resting 60 seconds between sets
  4. Track peak speed via radar if available
BenchmarkMax effort swings should be 8–12% faster than baseline. After contrast, 80% swings should feel effortless.

Start here New to this category? Build your Yardage Book first — you cannot practice distance control until you know your actual carry numbers. Then move to the 10-Yard Ladder to develop feel around those numbers.

Setup

  • Ball center to slightly back in stance depending on trajectory needed
  • Hands ahead of the ball at address — shaft lean toward the target
  • Weight 55–60% on the lead side at address; do not shift back
  • Feet square or slightly open to promote rotation through impact

Feels

  • Compress the ball — feel the divot starting at or after the ball, never before
  • Maintain shaft lean through impact; lead wrist flat, trail wrist bent
  • Smooth acceleration into the ball — never decelerate or guide
  • Chest rotates to face the target in the follow-through, not just the arms swinging

Avoid

  • Scooping — flipping the trail hand under to "help" the ball up
  • Hanging back on the trail side through impact; kills compression
  • Aiming feet at the flag when trouble short or right demands a different play
  • Ignoring the miss direction — always plan for where your bad shot goes

Drills

Approach
Balls30+
Time30 min
LocationRange + Monitor

Hit 5 balls with every iron and wedge, recording the average carry with each. This is your personal yardage book — the numbers that reflect your actual swing, not the back of the box. Most golfers overestimate their distances by 10–15 yards and it costs them shots every round.

Steps

  1. Start with your gap wedge and work through to your longest iron
  2. Hit 5 quality shots with each club — discard any that feel clearly mishit
  3. Record the average carry (not total distance) for each
  4. Note your gaps: each club should be 8–12 yards apart. Identify clubs too close together or too far
  5. Repeat quarterly — your numbers change as your swing evolves
BenchmarkClean 8–12 yard gaps between every club. No two clubs within 5 yards of each other.
Balls20
Time20 min
LocationRange

Pick one club and hit shots to four targets exactly 10 yards apart: for example, 100 / 110 / 120 / 130. Trains precision distance control rather than swinging at one number repeatedly. The gaps are where you build feel.

Steps

  1. Pick a club and identify four targets 10 yards apart on the range
  2. Hit 5 balls to each target in sequence — no repeating a distance
  3. Use swing length changes, not grip changes: same grip, vary backswing length and pace
  4. Grade: how many of 20 balls finish within 5 yards of the target?
Benchmark14 of 20 balls within 5 yards of target. Mid/low HC target: 16/20.
Balls25
Time20 min
LocationRange

Take one wedge and hit it to 5 different distances using only swing length and tempo — no grip adjustments. This is the foundation of wedge feel. High and mid handicap players usually skip this entirely and wonder why their wedge game is inconsistent.

Steps

  1. Choose a 52 or 56 degree wedge
  2. Set targets at 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, and 100% of your full carry with that club
  3. Hit 5 balls to each distance. Vary only backswing length (9 o'clock, 10 o'clock, full)
  4. Count how many balls stop within 10 yards of each target
Benchmark18 of 25 balls within 10 yards of target. Builds to 20/25 as feel develops.
Balls20
Time25 min
LocationRange

Pick the flag as your target — not a zone, not "somewhere on the green." Commit to an exact carry number and execute. This drill removes the comfortable vagueness that mid-handicap players use to protect themselves from expectation. Strokes gained approach rewards proximity to the hole, not proximity to the green.

Steps

  1. Select a specific flag at a known distance (use a rangefinder)
  2. Commit to your exact carry number before each shot — say it aloud
  3. Hit 20 balls, grading each: inside 15 feet / 15–30 feet / 30+ feet
  4. Track your "attack number" — how often did your ball finish pin-high?
Benchmark6 of 20 inside 15 feet. Low/scratch target: 8 of 20. Pin-high rate above 60%.
Balls24
Time30 min
LocationRange (windy day)

Practice deliberate club selection adjustments in wind. Into a headwind, take more club and swing at 80% — a smooth 7-iron beats a forced 8-iron every time. Downwind, take less club and let the ball run. Crosswind, play the shot shape that rides the wind rather than fighting it.

Steps

  1. On a windy range day, identify wind direction and estimate speed (light / moderate / strong)
  2. Hit 8 balls into the wind: club up one full club and swing at 80%
  3. Hit 8 balls downwind: club down one, allow for extra run
  4. Hit 8 balls in a crosswind: either shape into the wind or ride it — choose intentionally
  5. Track which group held the target better
BenchmarkCarry within 10 yards of intended number in each wind direction. No more mis-clubbing by a full club.
Balls30
Time25 min
LocationRange

Build one repeatable shot shape with your 7-iron that you trust completely. Tour players don't hit every shot differently — they have a stock flight they default to under pressure. Yours should be a gentle fade or draw with a consistent trajectory. Once you have it, protect it.

Steps

  1. Choose a shot shape: gentle fade (most common reliable shape) or controlled draw
  2. Set up and aim to produce that shape on every single shot, no exceptions
  3. Hit 30 balls with the 7-iron, all intended to produce the same flight
  4. Grade: how many produced the intended shape? How tight was the dispersion?
  5. Once grooved, repeat with your 6-iron and 8-iron using the same shape
Benchmark24 of 30 producing the intended shape. Dispersion pattern should be tight and predictable, not scattered.

Start here New to this category? Start with the Dollar Bill Drill — if your low point is behind the ball, every other short game drill will fail. Once you're hitting it clean, move to the 6-Foot Circle to build green-side feel.

Setup

  • Narrow stance, feet 6–8 inches apart; lead foot flared slightly open
  • Weight 60–65% forward at address and keep it there throughout
  • Hands ahead of the ball — shaft lean toward the target at address
  • Ball position varies: center for bump-and-run, slightly back for lower trajectory

Feels

  • Arms and shoulders moving as one connected unit — no independent wrist flip
  • Chest rotates through; the club never passes the hands before impact
  • Contact the ball first, then the turf — feel the ball compressing before the brush
  • Follow-through matches backswing length — even tempo, not deceleration

Avoid

  • Scooping — trying to lift the ball by flipping the trail wrist under
  • Decelerating into impact; the most common cause of chunked and thinned chips
  • Excessive wrist hinge — this is an arms-and-shoulders motion, not a wrist motion
  • Using the same shot for every lie; tight lies and fluffy lies demand different setups

Drills

Short Game
Balls6
Time15 min
LocationPractice green

Place 6 balls in a circle around a hole at 6 feet — one at each clock position. The goal is to chip all 6 within tap-in range before moving on. The circle removes directional bias and forces you to handle uphill, downhill, and sidehill chips in the same session. The best short game drill in golf.

Steps

  1. Set 6 balls at 12, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 10 o'clock positions — all 6 feet from the hole
  2. Chip each ball; goal is to stop within 3 feet of the hole
  3. If you fail two in a row, restart the circle from the beginning
  4. Progress: complete the circle without a restart, then move out to 10 feet
BenchmarkComplete the full circle with all 6 within tap-in range. Progress to 10 feet when you can do it 3 sessions in a row.
Balls20
Time15 min
LocationChipping area

Place a dollar bill flat on the ground directly behind your ball. Your clubhead must not touch the bill on the downswing. It forces the low point of the swing arc forward — past the ball — which is the single most important thing in chipping. If you're chunking chips, this drill fixes it within a session.

Steps

  1. Place the ball on the practice green; lay a dollar bill touching the back of the ball
  2. Set up with hands forward, weight forward
  3. Chip without the club touching the bill on the way down
  4. A touched bill means the low point is behind the ball — adjust weight and hand position forward
Benchmark20 consecutive chips without touching the bill. Once there, remove the bill and feel the same motion carry over.
Balls20
Time20 min
LocationPractice green

Hit chips from 4 positions around a green — 12, 3, 6, and 9 o'clock — all at the same distance from the hole. Each position presents a different slope, grain, and trajectory challenge. This is how you build an all-around short game instead of only being comfortable from your favorite angle.

Steps

  1. Set up 4 balls at the same distance (15–20 yards) from a hole, at each clock position
  2. Hit 5 chips from each position; adjust for slope and lie as needed
  3. Score: 3 points inside 3 feet, 1 point inside 6 feet, 0 outside
  4. Target: 40 points out of a possible 60 to pass; 50+ is excellent
Benchmark40 of 60 points. Mid/low target: 48 of 60. Identifies your weakest approach direction.
Balls20
Time15 min
LocationFirm chipping area

Chip 20 balls from a tight, bare lie using only your 56-degree wedge. Most high handicappers are terrified of tight lies — this drill removes the fear through repetition. The key is committing fully: no deceleration, no scooping. A confident swing from a tight lie is actually more reliable than a tentative swing from rough.

Steps

  1. Find a tight, firm lie on the practice area or a worn patch of turf
  2. Play ball slightly back, weight forward, hands well ahead
  3. Commit to a firm, accelerating stroke — trust the loft on the club
  4. Count how many of 20 make clean contact. Chunked or thinned shots = the scoop crept in
Benchmark16 of 20 clean contacts from a tight lie. Below 12 means the low point is still behind the ball — revisit the Dollar Bill Drill first.
Balls20
Time20 min
LocationPractice green

Take only your 56-degree wedge and chip to holes at 5, 10, 20, and 30 yards. No other clubs allowed. Forces you to learn how swing length and trajectory control distance rather than switching clubs. Most high and mid handicappers have too many "chip clubs" and not enough feel with any of them.

Steps

  1. Identify four holes at different distances from your chipping area
  2. Hit 5 chips to each hole using only the 56-degree
  3. Vary trajectory by changing ball position and swing length — same club, different shots
  4. Grade proximity: how many stop within 6 feet of each hole?
Benchmark12 of 20 within 6 feet across all distances. Builds to 15/20 as feel develops over 4–6 sessions.
Balls20
Time25 min
LocationPractice area

Hit 5 chips from each of four lie types in sequence: thick rough, tight/hardpan, uphill slope, downhill slope. Simulates the variety you face on course where you rarely get a perfect lie. Elite short games are built on handling bad lies well, not perfect lies perfectly.

Steps

  1. Find or create four distinct lie conditions in your practice area
  2. Hit 5 balls from each lie, same target each time
  3. Adjust setup for each: rough = wider stance, more wrist; hardpan = hands forward, firmer grip; uphill = lean into slope; downhill = lean with slope, ball back
  4. Track proximity from each lie type — your worst lie reveals your next practice priority
BenchmarkAll four lie types producing shots within 10 feet. No lie type should be dramatically worse than the others.
Balls18
Time20 min
LocationPractice bunker

Hit 6 bunker shots to each of three distance zones: short (10 yards), medium (20 yards), and long (30 yards). Most players only practice getting out — they never learn to control distance. The key variable is sand entry point and swing speed, not loft changes.

Steps

  1. Set targets at 10, 20, and 30 yards from the bunker
  2. For short shots: enter the sand 3 inches behind the ball, abbreviated follow-through
  3. For long shots: enter 2 inches behind, full follow-through, more speed
  4. Hit 6 to each target; count balls landing within 5 yards of each zone
Benchmark4 of 6 within 5 yards of each target zone. Priority: exit the bunker on every single attempt before worrying about distance.

Start here New to this category? Run the Gate Drill first to understand your stroke path and face angle. Then do the Lag Ladder — most 3-putts are a distance control problem, not a line problem.

Setup

  • Eyes directly over the ball or slightly inside the target line — not outside
  • Putter face square to intended start line at address; grip pressure 3/10
  • Even weight distribution, slight knee flex, back straight — no slouch
  • Ball position slightly forward of center to promote a slightly ascending strike

Feels

  • Shoulders rocking like a pendulum — arms and wrists passive, not driving the stroke
  • Backswing and follow-through are the same length; never shorter on the follow-through
  • Feel the putter head weight swinging freely — don't muscle it, let it go
  • Pick a specific entry point on the hole, not "the hole." Roll the ball to that spot

Avoid

  • Steering — guiding the putter toward the hole instead of swinging through
  • Looking up early; wait to hear the putt drop, don't watch it travel
  • Decelerating into short putts — the most common cause of misses inside 6 feet
  • Fixating on hazards or trouble when reading; plan your line, then commit fully

Drills

Putting
Balls20
Time15 min
LocationPractice green

Place two tees in the ground just wider than your putter head, about 6 inches in front of the ball on your intended line. Roll putts through the gate without touching either tee. Any contact reveals exactly what's wrong: hitting the lead tee means the face is open, hitting the trail tee means it's closed or the path is off. Instant, objective feedback with no launch monitor needed.

Steps

  1. Set two tees just wider than your putter head, 6 inches ahead of the ball on your line
  2. Putt from 6 feet; the ball must pass through the gate cleanly
  3. Track how many of 20 roll through without contact
  4. Progress: move gate to 3 inches ahead of ball (harder), then add a second gate near the hole
Benchmark18 of 20 through the gate cleanly from 6 feet. Below 14 means path or face angle needs immediate attention.
Balls10
Time30 min
LocationPractice green

Hit 100 putts from 3 feet, tracking how many you make. No free passes, no mulligans — every miss counts. This is the most honest assessment of your short putting stroke available. The goal is to expose what happens when you get tired and when pressure accumulates over a long session. Most golfers never do this and never find out where their stroke actually breaks down.

Steps

  1. Mark a spot 3 feet from the hole; use a tee as a consistent marker
  2. Hit 10 putts, record how many drop, move to the next ball
  3. Repeat 10 rounds for 100 total putts
  4. Note when misses cluster — usually putts 60–80 when fatigue sets in
Benchmark85/100 for high HC. Mid: 90/100. Low/scratch: 95/100. Track over time — consistency matters more than one good session.
Balls8
Time15 min
LocationPractice green

Place 8 balls in a circle around the hole at 4 feet — one at each compass point and halfway between each. You must make all 8 consecutively. Miss one, restart from the beginning. This drill exposes every different break you'll face on a real green and trains you to handle pressure on each individual putt — because by putt 7 and 8, you feel it.

Steps

  1. Set 8 tee markers in a circle 4 feet from the hole
  2. Go around in order; all 8 must drop consecutively
  3. Miss any putt: restart from putt 1
  4. Progress: move to 5 feet once you complete it 3 times per session
BenchmarkComplete the full circle without a restart. Progress to 5 feet when you can do it twice in one session.
Balls15
Time20 min
LocationPractice green

Hit 5 putts to each of three distance zones — short (15 ft), medium (30 ft), and long (50 ft) — with a goal of finishing every ball within a 3-foot circle of the hole. Lag putting is responsible for more 3-putts than any other factor. Distance control, not line, is the primary skill for putts beyond 20 feet.

Steps

  1. Mark three zones at 15, 30, and 50 feet from a hole
  2. Hit 5 putts from each zone; goal is stopping within 3 feet of the hole
  3. Count how many of 15 total finish inside the 3-foot circle
  4. Worst zone becomes your next session's priority
Benchmark12 of 15 inside 3 feet. High HC acceptable start: 9/15. Track your worst zone each session.
Balls20
Time20 min
LocationPractice green

Hit 20-foot putts from 4 clock positions around the hole — 12, 3, 6, and 9 o'clock. Each position presents a different amount of break. The goal isn't to make them; it's to finish every ball within 18 inches of the hole regardless of direction. High and mid handicap players lose the most strokes to 3-putts, and poor pace control from mid-range is the cause.

Steps

  1. Set markers at 20 feet from a hole in each of 4 directions
  2. Hit 5 putts from each position — read the break before each
  3. Pace goal: finish within 18 inches. Making it is a bonus
  4. Count how many of 20 are inside 18 inches. Track across sessions
Benchmark15 of 20 within 18 inches. Below 10 means pace control is the primary issue — prioritize Lag Ladder before this drill.
Balls15
Time25 min
LocationPractice green

Find a 20-foot breaking putt on the practice green. Before hitting a single ball, map it: where is the apex of the break, what is the entry angle into the hole, and how much speed does this putt require? Write it down. Then hit 15 putts to test your read. This is how tour players build green reading intuition — deliberate analysis before execution, not guessing.

Steps

  1. Find a putt with 6+ inches of visible break
  2. Walk the line: mark the apex with a tee, estimate entry angle
  3. Write down your read before hitting any balls
  4. Hit 15 putts; compare outcomes to your initial read — adjust the tee if needed
  5. Repeat with a different putt; over time, compare your reads to reality
BenchmarkRead matches actual break within 1 ball-width on 10 of 15 attempts. Consistent misreads in one direction reveal a systematic bias to correct.
Balls15
Time15 min
LocationPractice green

Putt from 8 feet with your eyes closed after committing fully to your line. Close your eyes at address, make the stroke, and listen. If you're steering the putter toward the hole visually, this drill exposes it immediately. The ball flight tells you everything about your true stroke path and face angle, unfiltered by compensations.

Steps

  1. Read an 8-foot straight or slightly breaking putt, commit to your line
  2. Close your eyes at address before starting your stroke
  3. Make your normal stroke without opening your eyes; let the ball go
  4. Open eyes only after you hear the result — then assess where the ball finished and why
Benchmark8 of 15 within 12 inches of the hole. Consistent misses in one direction reveal path or face bias. Compare to eyes-open results for the same putt.

Start here New to this category? Begin with the Round Audit — you cannot fix mental patterns you haven't identified yet. Do it after your next 3 rounds before touching any other drill here.

Routine

  • Pre-shot routine: 2–4 steps maximum, identical every single time
  • Visualize the shot before stepping into your address — see it, then do it
  • Pick one specific target, not a zone or a general area
  • Once committed, execute without re-evaluating — doubt lives in the address position

On-Course Mindset

  • Process over outcome — your job on each shot is to execute the process, not control the result
  • One shot at a time; the scorecard doesn't exist until the round is over
  • Trust your practice — course is execution mode, not fix-it mode
  • After a mistake, give yourself 10 seconds then let it go completely

Avoid

  • Results-based thinking during execution — you control the process, not the ball
  • Comparing your game to your playing partners mid-round
  • Trying to fix your swing on the course — one simple thought maximum
  • Dwelling on previous holes; what's done is done, this shot is all that matters

Drills

Mental
BallsNone
Time15 min post-round
LocationAnywhere

Within 2 hours of finishing a round, review it by decision quality — not score. For each hole, ask: did I pick the right target? Did I commit? Did I execute my pre-shot routine? Score your decisions 1–3 regardless of outcome. A well-executed shot that produces a bad result still gets a 3. A poorly-executed shot that gets lucky gets a 1. Over time this converts post-round frustration into actionable patterns.

Steps

  1. Write down 3 holes where a mental mistake cost you a shot or two
  2. Write down 3 holes where your decision-making was sharp, regardless of score
  3. Score each decision 1 (poor), 2 (acceptable), 3 (excellent)
  4. Calculate your "decision score" — track it over 10 rounds to find patterns
  5. Identify one specific habit to change before the next round
BenchmarkAverage decision score above 2.2 over 10 rounds. Consistent 1s in the same situation reveal your most important mental pattern to fix.

Before every round on a course you know, spend 10 minutes mapping your intended play on each hole — specifically your miss direction and what shot you'll hit from the fairway if you miss short, long, left, or right. Decision fatigue on the course is real. Players who have pre-planned their responses to bad positions make far better recovery decisions than those who improvise under pressure.

Steps

  1. On each hole, write your primary tee shot target and preferred miss direction
  2. Identify the one place you cannot miss — make this your anti-target, not your target
  3. For your approach: which side of the green gives the easiest chip or putt if you miss?
  4. During the round, execute the plan without re-evaluating unless something unexpected changes
BenchmarkStick to your pre-planned decision on 14+ of 18 holes. Track how often deviation from the plan leads to higher scores.

Build a personal 4-step reset protocol for after bad shots: (1) allow a brief emotional reaction — 5 seconds, get it out; (2) take one deep breath and physically release tension by shaking your hands; (3) say your trigger phrase ("next shot" or "reset" — pick one and never change it); (4) execute your full pre-shot routine on the very next shot as if nothing happened. Practice this sequence on the range by deliberately hitting bad shots and resetting.

Steps

  1. Choose your personal trigger phrase — one word or two, said consistently
  2. On the range, hit a deliberate bad shot (aim way off target, mishit intentionally)
  3. Run the full 4-step protocol: react → breathe → trigger word → routine
  4. Evaluate: did the next shot quality match your baseline? It should
  5. Repeat 10 times per session until the protocol is automatic under pressure
BenchmarkShot quality after a reset equals your baseline quality on 8 of 10 attempts. The protocol fails if you're still thinking about the previous shot during the next swing.

Play a 9-hole round with self-imposed stakes and consequences on every shot. Examples: one mulligan for the entire 9 holes, lost ball means a two-stroke penalty played from where it entered trouble, any 3-putt means a push-up or extra chip after the round. Consequences don't have to be dramatic — they just have to be real enough that each shot matters. This is the only way to practice decision-making under actual pressure.

Steps

  1. Set your personal consequence before teeing off — write it down
  2. Play each hole as if it's the last hole of a match you're 1-up on
  3. Apply the One-Shot Reset Protocol after every mistake
  4. After the round, complete a Round Audit on decision quality
  5. Track your "pressure score" vs. your normal practice round score
BenchmarkPressure round score within 3 shots of your normal round average. Greater gap means mental game is the primary area of improvement.
