The 18th — a risk-reward par 5 with water guarding the green — has decided more than a few results. This is a field-quality week, $20 million, and 700 FedExCup points on the table.

LAST WEEK
Zurich Classic: The Fitzpatrick Brothers and a Four-Shot Lead That Almost Wasn't
Going into Sunday, the Fitzpatricks led by four. By the time they reached the 15th hole, that lead was gone. A double bogey on 12 followed by a bogey on 14 opened the door for two teams — Norwegians Kristoffer Reitan and Kristoffer Ventura and Americans Alex Smalley and Hayden Springer — who were already in the clubhouse at 30-under. Three teams deadlocked at the top of a PGA Tour event, with the leader still on the course. That's the kind of finish the format was designed to produce, and it delivered.
What separated the Fitzpatricks wasn't the front nine form they'd built the week on. It was Matt's shot from the bunker on 18 — a ball-first strike from sand that stopped a foot from the hole. That left Alex, 27, standing over a tap-in with a PGA Tour card on the line. He made it. Their final total of 31-under 257 set a tournament record.
The historical footnote is significant: the Fitzpatricks became the 13th pair of brothers to each claim a PGA Tour victory and the first to do so in over 20 years — either together or separately. Alex, who was ranked 141st in the world entering the week and seventh in the Race to Dubai, now has full PGA Tour membership through 2028, entry into remaining Signature Events, a spot at next month's PGA Championship, and a place in next year's Players Championship. One putt from two feet.
For Matt, the note worth filing: three wins in a single spring, including the 2022 U.S. Open champion now matching Jim Barnes as the only English players to win three or more PGA Tour events in a calendar year. Barnes did it in 1916, 1919, and 1921. The company is odd but the company is elite.
LEADERBOARD CONTEXT Fitzpatrick / Fitzpatrick: –31, won. Tournament record. The back-nine collapse and recovery compressed what looked like a comfortable win into a one-shot finish. That's the format working as intended — alternate shot under pressure, closing holes, two players sharing every mistake. Reitan / Ventura and Smalley / Springer: –30, T2. Both teams finished in the clubhouse and watched. No team further back came close. The window opened on 12 and shut on 18. Springer and Smalley, in particular, had the look of a team that could have won a normal stroke-play event this week. Alex Fitzpatrick's context. His first career win came six weeks ago at the Hero Indian Open on the DP World Tour. He was ranked 141st in the world. His brother, ranked 3rd, came in as the field's obvious anchor. By the end, Matt said he was "doing zero to help" on the back nine and Alex was "unbelievable." Accurate accounting.

Korda at the Chevron: The Margin Was Never in Doubt, and That Was the Story
Nelly Korda led the Chevron Championship wire-to-wire, finished at –18 (270), and won by five shots. The margin was the largest at this major in 18 years. No one got within four strokes of her the entire weekend. By conventional metrics, it wasn't close. By Korda's own account, it was one of the more mentally demanding rounds she's played.
The reason: she started Sunday with a five-shot lead and had to figure out how to play with one. Protect it too much, and you play defensive golf that deteriorates on contact. Chase birdies, and you're playing outside your process. Korda chose a middle path — playing conservatively to safe areas of greens, making two birdies early, adding two more late — but the short putts she'd missed in Round 3 stayed in her head. She made the important ones. She missed a few others. She won by five anyway.
This is her third major championship, returning her to world No. 1 for the first time since August. In five tournaments this year she's played in the final group every time: two wins, three runner-ups. The field in those five events didn't give her an easy path. She took it anyway. Ruoning Yin and Patty Tavatanakit finished T2. Yin went 56 consecutive holes without a bogey until the 17th. It didn't matter.
BY THE NUMBERS –18 (270). One shot outside Dottie Pepper's 72-hole Chevron record, set in 1999. Korda was playing conservatively the final two rounds with the lead, so the near-record was incidental rather than a target. 17th LPGA win, 21st worldwide. She joined Juli Inkster (1989) and Amy Alcott (1991) as the only players in the past 50 years to win LPGA majors while leading by multiple shots after every round. That's a short list. First American with 3 majors since Meg Mallon in 2000. She is 27. Context for where this season might be heading.
STAT OF THE WEEK

Years since brothers last shared a PGA Tour victory — together or separately
The Fitzpatricks are the 13th pair of brothers to each win on the PGA Tour. But the historical record is more specific than that: no brothers had won on tour — individually or as a pair — in more than 20 years before Sunday. That's not a warm narrative flourish. It's a data point that tells you something about how exceptional individual PGA Tour wins are in the first place, and how rare the combination of talent required at that level actually is within a single family.
Alex Fitzpatrick's path to Sunday is worth understanding. He was a standout amateur, turned professional, ground through the minor tours, earned and lost status multiple times, won his first professional title just six weeks ago in India, and arrived at the Zurich Classic ranked 141st in the world. His brother is ranked 3rd. The gap between them is enormous by any conventional measure. On Sunday, at TPC Louisiana, with a PGA Tour card on the line from two feet, the gap didn't exist.
IF YOU PLAY
What Korda Said About Missed Putts Is Worth Keeping
After the round, Korda said: "What I was telling myself was I really want to hoist this trophy, because I want to show the kids at home that it's OK to miss short putts and still win a major championship." She went on: "You're going to make mistakes. You have to mentally still be in it 100%."
This matters beyond the feel-good quote. Korda missed three putts inside four feet during the week. At the highest level of the women's game, with a five-shot lead, that's the kind of thing that compounds — each miss making the next attempt heavier. Instead of letting the misses define the round, she made the next decision, played the next hole, and won by five. The technical failure and the mental response to it were entirely separate events.
Most amateurs have the opposite problem at a smaller scale: a chunked chip on 4 becomes a hurried approach on 5 becomes a jabbed putt on 6. The mistake bleeds forward. The practical application isn't complicated — build a reset between shots that is identical whether the previous shot was good or bad. The same routine, the same target selection, the same pace. The shot that just happened isn't available to you anymore. The one you're standing over is.
