MASTERS RECAP
2026 Masters — What the Data Actually Shows
McIlroy shot -12 for the week. The scorecard says he won with his putter (+0.59 SG: Putting for the tournament — solid, not exceptional). What actually won it was his short game: +1.39 SG: Around the Green over four rounds, the best figure of any player in the top six. Augusta's collection areas, the firm turf around the greens, the chip shots that look routine on television and aren't — McIlroy handled them better than anyone. The course-fit model weights ATG at 25%, equal to driving. It knew this mattered. The market underweighted it.
The more interesting number in the top 10: Russell Henley posted +2.40 SG: Approach for the week — the highest approach figure of any player in the visible leaderboard, including Scheffler. He finished T3 at -10. Why not higher? His SG: Off-the-Tee was +0.16 for the week and his putting was +0.14. He hit every green and couldn't make anything from distance, couldn't create any birdie looks from the tee. When the best approach week in the field still only gets you T3, it tells you how much Augusta extracts from every other part of the game simultaneously.

IF YOU PLAY
The Shot Augusta Punishes Most Is the One You Never Practice
McIlroy's week came down to tight-lie chips — balls that trickled into Augusta's collection areas and sat down on firm turf with no cushion underneath. That shot is uncomfortable for almost every amateur because practice facilities don't have it. Range mats and fluffy rough train you to slide the club under the ball. Tight lies punish that instinct immediately.
The fix is simple to describe and takes repetition to trust: weight forward, shaft leaning toward the target, and commit to the strike. No deceleration. The tight lie does not forgive a slowing clubhead — it rewards a decisive one. Find a patch of firm, short grass on your course and chip from it until the contact feels normal. That one adjustment does more for your scoring than almost anything you can work on from full swing distance.
