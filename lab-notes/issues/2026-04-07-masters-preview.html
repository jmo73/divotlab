MASTERS PREVIEW
Masters Tournament
Augusta National Golf Club · Augusta, GA · April 9–12
The first major of 2026 starts Thursday. Augusta National is the same every year — the same azaleas, the same Amen Corner, the same firm and fast putting surfaces that end careers in a single afternoon. Cut falls after 36 holes: top 50 players and ties advance. And yet every year, the same analytical question: who is built for this course?
Augusta rewards patience over aggression. It rewards approach play that leaves the ball below the hole. It rewards a short game that can handle firm turf and lightning-fast undulating greens — the chip shots that look simple and aren't. What the broadcast doesn't tell you: SG: Around the Green is as predictive of Masters outcomes as SG: Approach. The course punishes players who miss greens in the wrong spots, and getting up-and-down from those positions is an art form that only about 20 players on Tour have mastered.
The other structural fact: Augusta National is not a long hitter's course the way Bay Hill or Torrey Pines is. Driving distance matters — but driving accuracy matters more. The trees tightened significantly in the 2010s and the rough off fairways punishes tee shots that miss specific angles. Off the tee, it's a positioning course, not a power course. That distinction shapes the course-fit model significantly.

WHAT I GOT WRONG The Houston model missed badly, and it's worth explaining why rather than moving past it. Woodland's negative SG: ATG made him look poor in a balanced model. But Memorial Park that week played firm and fast, which rewards scrambling ability differently than the model weights. Nicolai Hojgaard finishing 2nd (+3.0% win probability) was more in line with what the model expected. PRACTICE LAB
The Downhill Chip Drill
A short game drill — inspired by Augusta's collection areas and firm surfaces
The Masters is decided around the greens. In the last five editions, the winner averaged +2.1 SG: Around the Green over 72 holes — better than any other category. The specific shots that matter at Augusta are not the easy chips from flat ground. They're the downhill lies, the tight lies on firm turf, and the shots that require landing on a specific slope and letting the ball release. Most amateurs practice chipping from rough or fluffy lies. That's not what Augusta or most well-conditioned courses ask for.
Setup: Find the firmest, fastest area of your chipping green. Place 10 balls in a downhill lie — you want the slope going away from you toward the hole. Set a target 15-20 feet away on a fast surface. Use your most-lofted wedge (58 or 60 degree).
Scoring: 3 points: within 3 feet. 2 points: within 6 feet. 1 point: on the green. 0: miss the green or blade it. 10 balls, max 30 points. Tour average for this shot type from testing is around 18-20. Getting to 15 consistently is genuinely solid.
The real lesson: Commit to landing the ball on a specific spot and letting the hill do the work. The amateur instinct is to try to stop the ball with the wedge — that produces fat shots or chunks. The Tour approach is to land it at the bottom of the slope and trust the release. Pick your landing spot before your backswing. Every time.
0–10 HCP
Add a second phase: after the standard drill, move the balls back 5 yards and try to land each one on a 2-foot square landing zone you've chosen. The point isn't distance — it's precision in landing spot selection. Tour players talk about "seeing the bounce" on fast chips. Practice identifying the optimal landing spot before committing to the shot. Target score: 22+.
10–20 HCP
Do the standard drill as written. Focus entirely on contact quality — the number one error is deceleration on firm lies, which produces the dreaded skull. Commit to accelerating through the ball even though the intuition is to be delicate. If you're blading, grip down an inch and narrow your stance. Target score: 14+.
20+ HCP
Simplify: find a flat chip shot 10-15 feet from the hole, not a downhill one. Practice landing the ball 1-2 feet in front of you and watching where it releases. The only goal is to get the ball on the green every time. Once you're hitting the green 8 out of 10 times from flat lies, move to a gentle slope. Getting on the green from around the green is worth more strokes saved than any full-swing improvement you can make. Target: 8/10 on the green.
IF YOU PLAY THIS WEEK
The single biggest thing amateurs can borrow from how Augusta is played: stop aiming at pins that are cut near trouble. Augusta has six or seven flags per round where the correct play is a full club short of the hole — the Tour players know this, the broadcast rarely shows it. On your course this weekend, pick three holes where the pin is tucked near a bunker or short side slope, and intentionally aim 15-20 feet away from it. Track your scores on those holes vs. the ones where you chase the flag. Strokes gained research shows mid-handicappers lose more strokes from short-side misses than any other single error pattern. Aiming away from the pin on tough hole locations is the one habit that will save you the most strokes for the least amount of range work.
